Jellyfish


Jellyfish are a group of aquatic animals that belong to the phylum Cnidaria. They are known for their unique appearance, with a bell-shaped body and long, trailing tentacles. These tentacles are armed with stinging cells called nematocysts, which jellyfish use to capture prey and defend themselves against predators. Jellyfish can be found in oceans all around the world, from shallow reefs to the deep sea, and they come in a wide range of shapes and sizes.


There are over 2,000 species of jellyfish, and they play an important role in marine ecosystems. They are a source of food for many animals, including sea turtles, fish, and birds. Jellyfish also help to keep the ocean's food web in balance by eating small planktonic organisms and competing with other predators for resources. However, some species of jellyfish can have negative impacts on human activities, such as clogging fishing nets or causing painful stings. Despite their potential risks, jellyfish are fascinating creatures that continue to intrigue scientists and ocean enthusiasts alike.
